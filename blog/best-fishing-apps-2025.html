There are dozens of fishing apps on the market in 2025, and choosing the right one can feel overwhelming. Some are bloated social networks disguised as fishing tools. Others are so barebones they barely do more than a notepad. And a surprising number require expensive monthly subscriptions for basic features.

We spent three months testing the seven most popular fishing log apps on real fishing trips ‚Äî bass fishing on lakes, trout fishing in streams, saltwater inshore trips, and ice fishing in the Midwest. This isn't a spec-sheet comparison. It's a field test. Here's what we found.

Why Use a Fishing App at All?

Before diving into the comparison, it's worth asking: why use an app instead of just keeping mental notes or a paper journal? The short answer is automation and analysis.

A good fishing app captures GPS coordinates, weather data, barometric pressure, and timestamps automatically. That's information that would take five minutes to manually record ‚Äî and most anglers won't bother with paper. The app captures it in the background while you focus on fishing.

The second advantage is searchability. After a year of entries, you can instantly filter your catches by species, location, season, weather conditions, or lure type. Try doing that with a paper notebook. As we discussed in our guide on keeping a fishing journal, the best journal is one you'll actually use ‚Äî and apps dramatically reduce the friction.

The third advantage is mapping. Seeing your catches plotted on a map reveals patterns that are invisible in list form. You'll notice clusters of catches near specific structure, seasonal migration patterns, and spots you've been ignoring that actually produce well.

That said, not all fishing apps are created equal. Let's look at what matters.

How We Evaluated

We judged each app on five criteria, weighted by what actually matters to a fishing angler on the water:

  • Catch logging speed (30%) ‚Äî How fast can you log a catch and get back to fishing? If it takes more than 60 seconds, you'll stop using it.
  • Offline functionality (25%) ‚Äî Many great fishing spots have no cell signal. An app that requires internet is useless when it matters most.
  • Data quality (20%) ‚Äî What environmental data does it capture automatically? GPS, weather, pressure, water temp?
  • Analysis tools (15%) ‚Äî Can you search, filter, and find patterns in your data? Charts, maps, stats?
  • Pricing fairness (10%) ‚Äî Is the pricing model reasonable, or does it nickel-and-dime you for basic features?

We'll be transparent ‚Äî this is our app. But we built it specifically because the other options frustrated us, and the field test confirmed that our approach works. Here's why.

Catch logging takes about 15 seconds. Open the app, tap "Log Catch," snap a photo, select the species, add optional notes, done. GPS location, weather data, barometric pressure, and timestamp are captured automatically. There's no mandatory social sharing, no upsell popups, no friction between you and your data.

The one limitation: it's a PWA, not a native app. That means you install it from the browser rather than the app store. For 95% of users this makes zero practical difference, but it's worth mentioning.

2. Fishbrain ‚Äî Best Social Features

Price: Free (limited) / $9.99/month Pro
Platforms: iOS, Android
Offline: Limited

Fishbrain is the largest fishing app by user count, and for good reason. Its social features are excellent ‚Äî you can see what other anglers are catching nearby, discover new spots based on community reports, and participate in a genuine fishing social network.

What it does well: The community data is Fishbrain's killer feature. Seeing that someone caught a 5-lb bass near your local lake yesterday, on a specific lure, is incredibly valuable intel. The species identification feature (snap a photo, AI identifies the fish) is clever and mostly accurate. The catch log itself is decent, with good photo support and basic condition tracking.

Where it falls short: The free version is severely limited ‚Äî you need Pro ($9.99/month, $59.99/year) for depth maps, advanced filtering, and ad-free use. That's $120/year for a fishing journal. Offline support is minimal; you need a connection for most features. And the social aspect, while fun, can be distracting when you just want to log a catch quickly.

Fishbrain also has a privacy concern for serious anglers: your catch data feeds the community map. If you've found a secret honey hole, logging it on Fishbrain effectively shares it with the world. You can set catches to private, but the default is public.

Bottom line: Great for social anglers who want community features and don't mind the subscription. Less ideal for anglers who prioritize privacy, offline use, or a simple catch journal.

3. ANGLR ‚Äî Best for Tournament Anglers

Price: Free (basic) / $4.99/month Pro
Platforms: iOS, Android
Offline: Partial

ANGLR positions itself as a fishing performance platform, and it delivers on that promise. The trip tracking is excellent ‚Äî it records your route on the water, showing not just where you caught fish but where you fished without catching. That path data is surprisingly useful for analyzing your approach.

What it does well: Route tracking is genuinely unique and valuable. The integration with popular electronics (Garmin, Lowrance) adds depth data to your entries. Tournament features like leaderboards and team management are well-implemented. The catch log captures detailed data including precise GPS waypoints.

Where it falls short: The interface feels cluttered, especially on smaller phones. There's a learning curve that will frustrate casual anglers. Offline support exists but is buggy ‚Äî we lost data twice during testing when syncing after an offline session. The free version is almost unusably limited, pushing you toward the monthly subscription.

Bottom line: If you fish tournaments or want detailed route/trip tracking, ANGLR is worth considering. For casual to intermediate anglers who just want a reliable catch journal, it's overkill.

4. Fishidy ‚Äî Best Map Features

Price: Free (limited) / $9.99/month Premium
Platforms: iOS, Android, Web
Offline: Map downloads only (Premium)

Fishidy focuses on maps, and its map experience is impressive. Detailed depth contours, vegetation maps, and structure markers make it a solid tool for pre-trip planning. You can mark waypoints, create custom maps, and overlay your catches on detailed bathymetric data.

What it does well: The maps are Fishidy's standout feature. If you fish large lakes and need detailed contour maps, the map quality rivals dedicated chartplotting apps. The ability to download maps for offline use (Premium only) is a nice touch. Catch logging is adequate, with standard fields for species, bait, conditions, and photos.

Where it falls short: The catch logging feels secondary to the map features. It's slower than dedicated logging apps and captures less automatic data. The $9.99/month price puts it in premium territory without premium journaling features. And the social features feel bolted-on rather than integral.

Bottom line: An excellent mapping tool with a decent catch log attached. If maps are your priority, it's worth a look. If catch logging and analysis are your priority, look elsewhere.

Pro Angler bills itself as an all-in-one fishing platform, combining catch logging, weather forecasting, solunar calendars, and tide charts. The weather integration is genuinely excellent ‚Äî it pulls detailed forecasts, barometric trends, and solunar predictions specific to your fishing location.

What it does well: Weather data is unmatched. The solunar calendar with activity predictions is well-implemented. Tide charts for saltwater anglers are detailed and accurate. The catch log captures good data and the interface is clean.

Where it falls short: Almost everything requires internet. Offline support is essentially nonexistent, which is baffling for a fishing app. The subscription price is steep for what amounts to weather + catch logging. Data export is limited or requires premium tier.

Bottom line: If you always fish with good cell signal and want integrated weather forecasting, Pro Angler delivers. For anyone who fishes remote areas (which is... most anglers), the lack of offline support is a dealbreaker.

TackleBox Journal is a simple, no-nonsense catch logging app that does one thing and does it reasonably well. It's the closest digital equivalent to a paper fishing journal ‚Äî straightforward entry forms, basic search, and no social features or subscription upsells.

What it does well: Simplicity. You can log a catch in under 30 seconds. The one-time price is refreshingly honest. Offline support is solid. The interface is clean and uncluttered, which longtime anglers appreciate.

Where it falls short: iOS only ‚Äî Android users are out of luck. Limited automatic data capture (no auto-weather, no barometric pressure). The map view is basic. Analysis tools are minimal ‚Äî you can browse entries but there's no real stats or pattern detection. No photo support in catch entries (added via notes only).

Bottom line: A good choice for iOS users who want the simplest possible digital fishing journal. But it leaves a lot of value on the table by not leveraging the phone's capabilities for automatic data capture.

Fish Rules is primarily a fishing regulations app, but it includes a basic catch logging feature. If your biggest need is knowing size limits, bag limits, and season dates ‚Äî and you want a simple log on the side ‚Äî it's a solid two-in-one solution.

What it does well: The regulations database is comprehensive and regularly updated. GPS-based regulation lookup (tells you the rules for wherever you're standing) is brilliant and genuinely useful. Species identification is good. The catch log is basic but functional.

Where it falls short: The catch log is clearly a secondary feature. No automatic weather capture, limited map features for catches, and no meaningful analysis tools. It's a regulations app that happens to log catches, not a catch logger that includes regulations.

Bottom line: Download it for the regulations (every angler should). But don't rely on it as your primary fishing journal.
